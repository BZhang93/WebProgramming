Web Programming

2013 Semester Group Project

Overview

You will be working on a team of 4 or 5 people (see below) to create a product pertaining to, or complementing, vehicles. Legitimate vehicles include planes, trains, automobile, and bikes. The foot does not constitute as a vehicle. Things complementing vehicles include stops and stations.

Goal of Semester Group Project

Have students work in a team to define a problem and build a solution.

Past Semester Group Project Themes

  • Fall 2011 (Web Engineering): Health
  • Spring 2011: Retail
  • Fall 2010: Personal Finance

Ideas

  • A way to promote sharing private parking spots in a parking lot with a limited number of spaces.
  • A rental system for private parking spots.
  • A bike rack locator.
  • A way to shame those who use objects (e.g., chairs) to save a parking space.

What You Cannot Do

  • A blog
  • A message board
  • A calculator of some sort
  • A website to help you find the nearest watering hole / liquor store / Starbucks / etc. Been there, done that.
  • A site to find directions. That's what Google Maps, MapQuest, etc. are for.
  • An MBTA subway or bus tracker. You essentially did that in Assignment 3!
  • A ride sharing application (including carpooling). This idea is too saturated and quite frankly, way too sketchy.
  • A shortest path finder. Ugh!

Absolute Requirements

The product that your team creates must adhere to the following non-negotiable requirements:

  1. The product must use HTML5.
  2. The product must use JavaScript, jQuery, and Ajax.
  3. The team must use GitHub for the project. Each team will be provided a private repo.
  4. The product must have desktop and mobile web user interfaces. That is, the user interface is different on the desktop than on mobile.
  5. The product must allow users to login and save some data.
  6. The product must allow users to "delete" their account with the option of resurrecting it in 30 days.
  7. The product must use server-side data persistence (e.g., with PostgreSQL, MongoDB, MySQL)
  8. The product must use a server-side framework or technology (e.g., Node.js, Ruby on Rails, PHP)
  9. The product must use least 2 APIs (e.g., Google Maps API, Twitter, Facebook, Evernote) with JSON and/or XML parsing

Pick Five

On top of the absolute requirements, the product must use (at least) five of the following technologies or ideas (your team's choice):

  • Geolocation
  • Front-end framework including Bootstrap
  • Data / screen scraping
  • File upload and sharing
  • Reporting with charts and graphs
  • Custom search. Simply incorporating a Google search box on your product is not allowed
  • Data storage in the clouds (e.g., Amazon AWS, Dropbox)
  • Send emails and/or SMSes (e.g., Twilio)
  • Client-side data persistence with either local storage or web SQL

Deliverables

  1. Project proposal and design doc (15 points; due on Thursday, Pi Day). In text format and using bullet-points:
    • Title of product
    • Problem statement (i.e., what is the problem?)
    • How do you solve the problem?
    • List of all the features that your team will implement (including which of the "Pick 5" items were chosen by the team). Your team will be held accountable for the features you list!
    • What data will your product be collecting and using
    • Any algorithms or special techniques that will be necessary
    • Electronic mockups of the product. No hand-drawn mockups accepted.
  2. Product checkup (15 points; due on Tuesday, April 9th)
  3. The completed product (30 points; due on Friday, May 3rd at 12 PM)
  4. Elevator pitch presentation (5 points; on Friday, May 3rd at 12 PM) - You have less than 5 minutes to explain your product to me and to the judges! This is your final exam block. Room and location TBD. Food will most likely be provided.

All works and ideas are owned by the members of the team. I cannot take ownership of any of your work. I do not have stake or equity in any project created in my classes for that matter.

Notes

  • I can add to the list of "Pick 5" or "Ideas" at any time.
  • Your team is allowed to change direction or scope of the project but your team will need to submit a new project proposal / design doc and no time extension will be given.

Assessment

Your team will be graded on five factors:

  1. Functionality - Something that works consistently. Remember, an app with fewer features that work is significantly better than one with numerous features, in which some either do not work or are buggy.
  2. Utility - Does your product fulfill a recognized need? Is it also easy to understand?
  3. Originality - C'mon, another Joey Tracker? Better if your team strives to do something more cosmic.
  4. Technical Competency - Use good programming practices, consistent git commits and pushes, meaningful git commit messages used.
  5. Teamwork - Does all team members contribute? Having one or two people doing all the work is not good. On the last day of class, Thursday, April 25th, every person will conduct a performance review on each teammate!
