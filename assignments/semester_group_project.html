Web Programming

2014 Semester Group Project

Overview

You will be working on a team of 4 or 5 people (see below) to create a prototype pertaining to personal finance. Please note that a prototype is different than a product: a product is a complete system with documentation while a prototype simulates most of the complete system. I do not expect a full-blown product to be completed by the end of the semester.

Goal of Semester Group Project

Have students work in a team to define a problem and build a solution.

Past Semester Group Project Themes

  • Spring 2013: Vehicles
  • Spring 2011: Retail
  • Fall 2010: Personal Finance

Ideas

  • Help consumers save money, manage their day-to-day spending.
  • Help consumers make money.
  • Help consumers live simpler and cheaper.
  • Provide consumers basic financial education.
  • Educate consumers about the financial instrument they are interested in buying or have bought.
  • Inventory products in each room of the house, including their brand, retail price, and place of purchase.
  • Help consumers make spending decisions. Example: should I rent or buy given my income?
  • Anything pertaining to mobile payment

What You Cannot Do

  • A blog
  • A message board
  • Something that help students save money on textbooks (been there and done that, too saturated)
  • A series of financial calculators
  • Freecycle

Absolute Requirements

Your team's project must adhere to the following non-negotiable requirements:

  1. Must use HTML5.
  2. Must use JavaScript, jQuery, and Ajax.
  3. The team must use GitHub for the project. Each team will be provided a private repo.
  4. Must have desktop and mobile web user interfaces. That is, the user interface is different on the desktop than on mobile.
  5. Must use a server-side framework or technology (e.g., Node.js, Ruby on Rails)
  6. Must use least 2 APIs (e.g., Google Maps API, Twitter, Facebook, LevelUp) with JSON and/or XML parsing

Pick Five

On top of the absolute requirements, the project must use at least 5 of the following technologies or ideas (your team's choice):

  • Geolocation
  • Server-side data persistence (e.g., with PostgreSQL, MongoDB, MySQL)
  • Data storage in the clouds (e.g., Amazon AWS, Dropbox)
  • Client-side data persistence with either local storage or web SQL
  • Front-end framework including Bootstrap, Backbone.js, AngularJS
  • Data / screen scraping
  • Reporting with charts and graphs
  • Send emails and/or SMSes (e.g., Twilio)

Deliverables

  1. Project proposal and design doc (15 points; due on Thursday, March 13th). In text format and using bullet-points:
    • Project title
    • Problem statement (i.e., what is the problem?)
    • How do you solve the problem?
    • List of all the features that your team will implement (including which of the "Pick 5" items were chosen by the team). Your team will be held accountable for the features you list!
    • What data will your prototype be using and collecting
    • Any algorithms or special techniques that will be necessary
    • Electronic mockups of what your team will be developing using wireframes. No hand-drawn mockups accepted.
  2. Project checkup (15 points; due on Thursday, April 10th)
  3. The completed project (30 points; due on Thursday, May 1st at 12 PM)
  4. Elevator pitch presentation (5 points; on Thursday, May 1st at 12 PM) - You have less than 5 minutes to explain your project to me and to the judges! Room and location TBD. Food will most likely be provided.

Intellectual Property

All works and ideas are owned by the members of the team. I cannot take ownership of any of your work. I do not have stake or equity in any project created in my classes for that matter.

Notes

  • I can add to the list of "Pick 5" or "Ideas" at any time.
  • Your team is allowed to change direction or scope of the project but your team will need to submit a new project proposal / design doc and no time extension will be given.

Assessment

Your team will be graded on five factors:

  1. Functionality - Something that works consistently. Remember, a project with fewer features that work is significantly better than one with numerous features, in which some either do not work or are buggy.
  2. Utility - Does your project fulfill a recognized need? Is it also easy to understand?
  3. Originality - C'mon, another product comparison clone? Better if your team strives to do something more cosmic.
  4. Technical Competency - Use good programming practices, consistent Git commits and pushes, meaningful Git commit messages used.
  5. Teamwork - Does all team members contribute? Having one or two people doing all the work is not good. On the last day of class, Thursday, April 24th, every person will conduct a performance review on each teammate!
